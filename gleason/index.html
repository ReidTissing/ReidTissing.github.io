Getting to Know Andrew Gleason

Where’d your interest in dog training come from?

"We always had dogs growing up. When I was a kid I had a traumatic encounter with a neighbor’s German Shepherd. I thought it would behave like my dogs - nice, friendly - but instead he attacked me. Sometime after that I started to look at my dogs differently with a sincere curiosity as to why they have different communication than we do, how to connect better, and have a relationship with a dog. As a child I suffered some intense trauma. Growing up, I felt like somewhat of an outcast. I wasn’t very trusting of people, but dogs provided a safe relationship for me. I began to really explore that connection with a sincere curiosity which really satisfied a lot of my relational needs. I learned so much about my human relationships from what I'd learned with dogs."

I have a real diversity of disciplines that is highly unique in dog training.

How did your initial interest grow into a career?

"My curiosity and practice evolved into training friends’ dogs, working for different trainers, and apprenticeships. I had other jobs, sure, but I was always training on the weekends. I opened my first formal business in 2011. I reached a point in my mid-20's where I thought I knew all there was to know (I think most of us have reached that point). Now that I’m moving into my mid-30's I’m being open-minded about things I had formerly dismissed and not even investigated. I decided to sit down and learn; I don't throw anything out. There's a use for everything out there. I try and incorporate new philosophies."

What’s unique about your approach?

"Since opening my own business over a decade ago (wow, time flies!), it’s always been really important for me to keep up with new training methods and ideas. I’m always reading, always learning from somebody. I go to several nationwide workshops a year. The methodology I use is pretty unique - I’m the only trainer certified in it in the Southern U.S. I focus on addressing the issues dogs have when trying to navigate our world. When they don’t understand or can’t communicate, they act out. My emphasis is on transforming the way dogs understand and interact. Once that happens, it stops the problem at the source. If you just force a dog not to bark, they’re just going to express that anxiety or frustration in another way. I think of it as treating the cause rather than the symptom. I want owners to be on the same page as their dog - that’s the goal. Through my experience in training pet dogs, sport dogs, working dogs, service dogs - I was always interested in 'I want to learn how to train that kind of dog'. From practicing so many very different methods in dogs I learned so many ways to train the same thing. As a result, I have a real diversity of disciplines that is highly unique in dog training."
